Activity manifolds across criticality regimes

This page treats the activity raster of a single reservoir as a cloud of points in a high-dimensional state space, and asks a geometric question: how does the shape of that cloud change between subcritical, near-critical and supercritical regimes?

To make the cloud visible, we use Isomap [1], a nonlinear dimensionality-reduction method that preserves geodesic distances along the data, rather than straight-line distances in the ambient space as PCA does.

From raster to point cloud

Let $s_t \in \{0,1\}^{N}$ be the binary state of an $N$-neuron reservoir at macro-step $t$. Recording $T$ steps gives a raster $R \in \{0,1\}^{N\times T}$. We treat each column as one sample, so a recording becomes a set of $T$ points in the discrete hypercube $\{0,1\}^{N}$. The empirical question is what subset of that hypercube is actually visited under each dynamical regime.

Isomap in three steps

Isomap recovers low-dimensional structure on the assumption that the data lie near a manifold $\mathcal{M}$ embedded in the ambient space. Pairwise ambient distances (Euclidean, Hamming, …) are reliable only locally; on a curved manifold, distant points should be compared by walking along $\mathcal{M}$ rather than across it.

1. $k$-nearest-neighbors graph. Build a graph $G$ in which every point is connected to its $k$ closest neighbors under an ambient distance $d$. For binary rasters we use the Hamming distance $d_H(s,s') = \sum_i \mathbb{1}[s_i \neq s'_i]$; the local-distance assumption is that for nearby states, $d_H$ is a good proxy for the true geodesic.

The parameter $k$ is the locality knob of the whole method, and the only place where the geometry of the data enters Isomap. Small $k$ keeps edges between truly similar states, so geodesics have to walk along the manifold one shell at a time — this is when Isomap actually does its job and recovers curvature that PCA cannot see. If $k$ is too small, however, $G$ disconnects and the method is only defined on its largest connected component (we report the size of that component in the stats panel). Large $k$ connects each point to a substantial fraction of the cloud, so most pairs are direct neighbors and graph distances start to coincide with straight-line ambient distances; in that limit the manifold-following property is lost and Isomap degenerates toward PCA. A common rule of thumb is to keep $k$ in the range $\log T \lesssim k \lesssim \sqrt{T}$, which for the sampling sizes used here means roughly $k \in [6,\,20]$.

2. Geodesic distances. The geodesic between two points is approximated by the shortest path in $G$:

$$D_{ij} \;=\; \min_{\pi:\,i \to j} \sum_{(u,v)\in\pi} d(u,v),$$

computed e.g. with Dijkstra from each source. Long-range distances now follow the manifold instead of cutting through empty ambient space.

3. Classical MDS. Embed the geodesic distance matrix into $\mathbb{R}^3$ by double-centering and taking the top three eigenvectors:

$$B \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2}\,J\,D^{(2)}\,J,\qquad J = I - \tfrac{1}{T}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top,$$

where $D^{(2)}$ is the entrywise-squared geodesic matrix. If $B$ has eigenpairs $\{(\lambda_k, v_k)\}$, the 3D Isomap coordinates are $Y = [\sqrt{\lambda_1}\,v_1,\ \sqrt{\lambda_2}\,v_2,\ \sqrt{\lambda_3}\,v_3]$.

PCA can be recovered as the special case where $D$ is the Euclidean distance matrix; the Isomap-vs-PCA difference is therefore entirely in the geodesic step.

Reading the embedding shape

Each point in the embedding has coordinates $(\sqrt{\lambda_1}\,v_{1,i},\ \sqrt{\lambda_2}\,v_{2,i},\ \sqrt{\lambda_3}\,v_{3,i})$, so the bounding box of the cloud is set entirely by the top three eigenvalues of $B$. Their relative magnitudes give a direct geometric reading:

  • $\lambda_1 \gg \lambda_2,\lambda_3$  →  filament (one dominant axis).
  • $\lambda_1 \approx \lambda_2 \gg \lambda_3$  →  plane (two comparable axes, one weak).
  • $\lambda_1 \approx \lambda_2 \approx \lambda_3$  →  3D ball / cloud (no axis dominates).

The variance ratio $\sum_{k\le3}\lambda_k\,/\,\mathrm{tr}(B)$ reported in the stats panel quantifies how well 3 dimensions summarize the geodesic geometry. A high ratio means the cloud you see is a faithful picture of the data; a low ratio means there is a lot of variance hidden in $\lambda_4,\lambda_5,\ldots$ that the 3D view cannot show — the cloud's shape is then itself a diagnostic, not the geometry.

What to expect across regimes

The micro rule is the same Beggs reservoir used elsewhere on this site (see the About page); only the branching ratio $\sigma = (N-1)\,p^{\mathrm{micro}}$ changes between regimes. Crucially, the weight matrix is homogeneous ($W_{ij} = p^{\mathrm{micro}}$ for $i\neq j$), so the dynamics is permutation-invariant in the neuron indices: the system only "knows" how many neurons are active, not which specific ones. The space of active states therefore decomposes into shells indexed by $n_{\mathrm{active}}$, and within each shell the visited patterns are statistically equivalent under permutation of neurons.

  • Subcritical ($\sigma < 1$): cascades die fast, so the recording is concentrated in a thin low-activity shell (mostly $n_{\mathrm{active}} \in \{1,2,3\}$). Within that shell the visited patterns are essentially uniformly random sparse subsets; pairwise Hamming distances concentrate around a single value ("concentration of measure"), so the geodesic matrix has many comparable eigenvalues and the embedding looks like an isotropic 3D cloud — a shadow of a high-dimensional ball.
  • Near-critical ($\sigma \approx 1$): cascades of all sizes coexist, so the recording spans a broad range of activity shells from $n_{\mathrm{active}} = 1$ up to whatever the largest cascade reached. This makes $\lambda_1$ much larger than the rest — its leading direction is essentially the activity level (and, secondarily, the cascade phase: growing vs. shrinking). The within-shell identity fluctuations split into many small, roughly equal eigenvalues $\lambda_3,\lambda_4,\ldots$. The result is the characteristic spectrum $\lambda_1 \approx \lambda_2 \gg \lambda_3$ and a clean planar embedding. This is the geometric fingerprint of criticality: away from $\sigma_c$ the dynamics fills its accessible state space roughly uniformly, while at $\sigma_c$ it organizes onto a lower-dimensional manifold.
  • Supercritical ($\sigma > 1$): symmetric to the subcritical case but on the opposite side of the hypercube. Activity saturates and the recording is concentrated in a thin high-activity shell; within that shell, identity fluctuations are again isotropic. The embedding looks like a 3D cloud, with a comparable-eigenvalue spectrum.

So the shape transition cloud → plane → cloud as $\sigma$ crosses $1$ is not an artifact of Isomap or of the sampling — it is a direct readout of the dimensionality of the dynamics. The variance-ratio stat typically peaks near $\sigma=1$, which means the 3D embedding is most faithful to the data exactly at criticality.

Caveats

The geodesic step assumes the data lie near a low-dimensional manifold in the first place. In the sub- and supercritical regimes that assumption is weak — the visited states fill a thin shell of the hypercube roughly isotropically, with no preferred low-dimensional structure — so the "cloud" embedding should be read as a diagnostic of that absence of structure rather than as a recovered manifold. The variance ratio in the stats panel typically drops in those regimes, which is the formal way of saying "the 3D picture is missing a lot of the variance".

When the $k$-NN graph $G$ is disconnected, Isomap is undefined globally; we report the size of the largest connected component and flag the rest in the stats panel. If you make $k$ too small the graph fragments; if you make it too large, Isomap degenerates toward PCA.

Isomap is also not the only choice. UMAP and diffusion maps would give different — and arguably more faithful — pictures for some of these regimes; they are good follow-ups once the Isomap baseline is solid.
